Hierarchical Experimentalist Agents

In-context skill evolution for LLM agents that actively experiment

Abstract

Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used to take action or augment human decision-making, yet most approaches rely on the model's parametric knowledge, possibly supplemented with fixed data or tools. This paradigm breaks down for novel domains or sophisticated queries that are not practically answerable from general prior knowledge. Instead, active experimentation may be needed to gather targeted, query-specific data in the domain.

We introduce Hierarchical Experimentalist Agents (HeXA), a novel in-context, experiment-centric reasoning approach that (1) iteratively designs and refines query-relevant experiments; (2) incrementally learns a library of reusable, composable skills that accelerate experimentation within and across tasks; and (3) integrates experimental data to effectively take actions or answer queries. HeXA self-improves in-context while co-evolving skills and strategies using interaction rewards — all without access to offline data, an optimal oracle, or teacher guidance.

We also introduce InterPhyre, a 2D procedural physics simulation environment with a tool-call and intervention API designed to make hypothesis generation and experimentation explicit and measurable. Using Claude Sonnet 4.6, HeXA reaches 64% success on the hardest level in InterPhyre, compared to 8% using a ReAct and 22% using a Reflexion prompting baseline. The agent also achieves 44% success by not doing any active experimentation and using only skills that were learned and transferred from easier levels.

Method

HeXA instantiates a two-agent architecture operating over a sequence of rounds. An actor generates trajectories by interacting with the environment through a tool-call interface, while an evolver reads batches of trajectories and distills them into a natural-language skill bank. At the start of each new episode, a retriever injects the top-confidence skills and common mistakes into the actor's context, so that each subsequent trajectory benefits from the accumulated experience of all prior ones.

Each trajectory is assigned a scalar reward reflecting both outcome and efficiency, providing the evolver with a richer learning signal than binary success alone. The evolver performs a two-pass distillation: first, contrastive skill extraction across the full batch; second, mistake and partial-skill extraction focused on the failure subset. Skills are merged, revised, or pruned across rounds, producing a form of meta-learning in which the agent learns not just strategies but how to learn strategies from interactions.

Beyond within-task learning, HeXA supports cross-task skill transfer: given source banks from one or more easier task families, the evolver identifies skills that encode structurally relevant principles, re-grounds them in the target task, and calibrates confidence based on how directly the principle transfers. This enables zero-shot transfer without target-task trajectories.

Results

On catapult, the hardest InterPhyre level, HeXA reaches 64.0% success with Claude Sonnet 4.6, compared to 2.0% for the no-tool Direct baseline, 8.0% for ReAct, and 22.0% for Reflexion. HeXA also reduces average iterations per episode from 22.9 (ReAct) and 21.3 (Reflexion) to 13.7 — a 40% reduction — indicating that the evolved skill bank makes each new episode genuinely easier to solve.

Skill banks transfer zero-shot to unseen levels. Combining fully-evolved banks from three source levels and synthesising a cross-level bank for catapult raises accuracy from 8.0% to 44.0% (+36 pp) with no target-level experimentation. The gains extend to open-weight solvers: HeXA improves Qwen-2.5-7B on down_to_earth from 62% to 72%, and lifts GPT-OSS-120B on catapult from 0% to 54%.

Against gradient-based GRPO fine-tuning of Qwen-2.5-3B at a matched 50-seed budget, HeXA outperforms GRPO by 4–8 points, illustrating the sample efficiency of in-context skill evolution in low-data regimes — though GRPO eventually catches up with sufficient training.
